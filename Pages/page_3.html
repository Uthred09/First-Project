Based On Style

Formal Upright Style (Chokan)

This form of bonsai resembles trees that are growing naturally in an uncrowded, open environment. The trunk line must taper from the base to the apex since it is vertical with the apex being over the center of the trunk base. The space between branches is less and each succeeding branch is shorter than those below it.

Informal Upright Style (Moyogi)

The most popular bonsai style is certainly this one. It shows a tree in the wild that has been harmed by the weather, with contorted branches and a trunk line. In the natural world, this shape is produced by the ongoing breaking of the trunk line caused by storms, ice, snow, or animal or insect activity.

Slanting Style(Shakan)

When observing trees in the wild, one frequently notices those that have been tipped to one side by the effects of wind or water, as well as those that lean at an angle in search of sunshine. To balance the weight of the tree's slant to the opposite side, these trees have evolved robust root systems on one side.

Semi-Cascade Style(Han-kengai)

This design is intended to represent a tree that is suspended from a cliffside by a stream or the ocean. The tree extends past the pot's rim and its trunk curves downward past the rim but not below the pot's base.

Full Cascade Style (Kengai)

Except that the cascading line of this bonsai style is lower than the container's base, it adheres to the semi-cascade principles. In order for the trunk line to extend as far, this necessitates placing the bonsai on a stand.

Double Trunk Style(Kengai)

In this design, a tree with two trunks is shown. Typically, two trees with various diameters' trunks have grown together at the base and are now fashioned as a single tree. Between the trunks, no branches may develop.
